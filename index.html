March 6, 2024

Current Research

For my dissertaion, I am continuing to work with Prof. Jason A. Dittmann on calculating occurrence rates within the Kepler field, with the main goal of finding the frequency of Earth-like planets around Sun-like stars. To do so, we are applying our single transit detection pipeline to all of Kepler, to recover new and old candidates. By approaching occurrence rates through single transit detections, we are able to push the detection threshold boundary for orbital period past the traditional Kepler pipeline, and hopefully populate the Earth-like regime with more candidates.

Currently, we are optimizing our pipeline to be an automated process with a low detection sensitivity. As an intermediate step, we have applied our pipeline to a subset of systems and have discovered four more candidates. All candidates are in transit timing variation (TTV) systems, increasing the new candidate's likelihood of being a true signal. Two of the four new candidates are single transit candidates, and the other two have periods greater than 500 days. We are currently working on dynamically modelling the systems and obtain follow-up observations when feasible.

March 6, 2024

Single Transit Detection Pipeline

For my Master's Thesis, I worked under the supervision of Prof. Jason A. Dittmann creating a single transit detection pipeline in Kepler with machine learning and onboard spacecraft diagnostics.

The search for Earth-like planets around Sun-like stars remains at the forefront of the exoplanet field. The Kepler yield loses sensitivity to planets towards longer periods and smaller planets, leaving a dearth of detections within the Earth-like parameter space. To probe longer orbital periods single transit detection algorithms are likely needed.

Our first paper, linked here, demonstrates that we are able to confidently detect long period exoplanets and that the engineering files of Kepler contains information useful in classifying transits. We report our first candidate planet of our pipeline, KOI 1271.02, which contains a single transit within Kepler and is responsible for the strong transit timing variations of its neighbor planet KOI 1271.01.

Check out the talk I gave for my Master's Defense detailing the pipeline and candidate detection.
